Here be dolphins...

Bottlenose dolphins are the archetypal dolphin, having gained fame from movies and their presense in aquatic parks. However, contrary to popular belief, they are not the most common dolphin worldwide. That title is owed to the common dolphin. Despite this they are one of the most widely studied species of dolphin and have been shown to be capable of communication through language, using tools, self regognition and the ability to pass knowledge on to the next generation which are products of their complex intelligence. Bottlenose Dolphins have a long history with humans, from driving fish into fishermen's nets to locating sea mines.

Orcas are the largest species of dolphin in the world. They inhabit a wide variety of regions, stretching from pole to pole. Their diets are also incredibly diverse, consisting of seals, sharks, whales and even other species of dolphins. Their nickname, the killer whale comes from ancient sailors who observed them hunting and killing whales. Orcas have been known to kill humans in captivity, where they are often kept in conditions damaging to their health, but never in the wild.

Spinner Dolphins are a small dolphin that inhabits mostly costal waters around the tropics. They are known for their acrobatic spins which they do as they swim across the waves. A dolphin can make up to seven spins in a single jump. Their scientific name, stenella longirostris means narrow long beaked dolphin, one of their key characteristics. River dolphins inhabit rivers all around the world. In the Amazon, there are five different species of river dolphins. Often recognised by their pink colourings, river dolphins can also be grey. Amazon river dolphins are the largest of the river dolphins and have long noses which they use to search for and hunt food amongst the river's vegitation. They have been known to take advantage of boat wakes to catch stunned prey. Bionic death dolphins are cybernetically enhanced bottlenose dolphins. They are equipped with a multitude of weapons including laser eyes and sword fins. These modifications and their extreme intelligence have allowed them to become the ultimate predators. However, their populaton remains very small, mostly due to the billion dollar expense of creating new cybernetic implants. Though originally designed to destroy submarines in the cold war, they are now rogue elements, patrolling the seas freely.
